The People Behind the Mic

About the Founders.

Two clinicians who got tired of talking about AI in medicine and started asking the people actually doing it.
Host I

Marcus Hale

Internist · Host
Affiliation
Department of Internal Medicine
Massachusetts General Hospital
Research focus
Clinical AI Integration
Cardiology ward decision systems
"I got curious about machine learning during a long night shift on a cardiology ward. A number on a screen that nobody could explain changed a decision. I wanted to know why."

Marcus Hale has spent fifteen years in internal medicine, most of them trying to understand why good doctors make bad decisions under pressure. When AI tools started appearing on his ward — flagging arrythmias, suggesting dosage adjustments — he didn't resist them or celebrate them. He started asking questions.

That curiosity became this show. He believes that the most important conversation in medicine right now isn't about capability — it's about trust, accountability, and what it means for a clinician to hand part of a decision to a machine they can't fully audit.

When he isn't recording, he teaches clinical reasoning to residents and reads more cardiology literature than anyone would call reasonable.

Priya Anand

Educator · Host
Affiliation
Department of Medical Education
University of California, San Francisco
Research focus
Clinical Reasoning
Diagnostic cognition & uncertainty
"Medical students learn to be confident. I try to teach them to be calibrated. There's a meaningful difference, and AI is making it more urgent."

Priya Anand teaches medical students by day and writes about clinical reasoning by night. Her work sits at the intersection of how people learn to think and what happens when those same people encounter tools that appear to already know the answer.

She came to this podcast not because she's optimistic about AI in medicine — but because she's curious, and because she thinks the questions being avoided are more interesting than the ones being answered.

Her writing on diagnostic uncertainty has been cited in clinical education programs across twelve countries. She is finishing a book about how doctors decide what they don't know.
